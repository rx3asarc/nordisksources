### Free Residual Chlorine in Bathing Water Reduces the Water-Holding Capacity of the Stratum
### Corneum in Atopic Skin
Taisuke Seki, Susumu Morimatsu, Hidefumi Nagahori and Masaaki Morohashi
Abstract
*Some patients with atopic dermatitis (AD) develop dry skin or exacerbated cutaneous inflammations with frequent swimming in public pools or after bathing. We examined the effects of residual chlorine in bathing water on the function of the stratum corneum (SC) in patients with AD and determined the lowest chlorine concentration showing an effect. In addition, we investigated the relationship between the free residual chlorine concen-tration in bathing water and the water-holding capacity of the SC in patients with AD. Twenty patients with AD and 10 normal control (NC) subjects were included in this study. The hydration status of the SC on the flexor surface of the forearm was measured with a corneometer before and after the subject’s arms were immersed in tubs filled with com-fortably hot water (40°C) containing residual chlorine at concentrations of 0, 0.5, 1.0 and 2.0 mg/L for 10 minutes in a room maintained at normal temperature (24°C) and relative humidity (55%). The water-holding capacity of the SC after immersion was calculated by integration of the hydration status determined every 30 seconds over a period of 10 min-utes. In the patients with AD, the average SC hydration status after immersion in comfort-ably hot water containing residual chlorine at 1.0 and 2.0 mg/L was significantly lower than that following immersion in water containing a negligible concentration of residual chlorine (i.e., less than 0.03 mg/L)(p <0.05). In the NC subjects, significant differences were observed only between the 2.0 mg/L and the negligible residual chlorine groups (p <0.05). The water-holding capacity of the SC was significantly decreased with a residual chlorine concentration of 0.5 mg/L or higher in the patients with AD (p <0.01). However, in the NC subjects, a significant decrease in water-holding capacity was observed only at a residual chlorine concentration of 2 mg/L (p <0.01). These results indicate, first, that the water-holding capacity of the SC in patients with AD is more sensitive to free residual chlo-rine exposure than that in NC subjects without AD. Second, these results suggest that free residual chlorine exposure in patients with AD may play a role in the development or ex-acerbation of AD.*

Materials and Methods Twenty patients with AD (AD group) and 10
non-atopic normal control subjects (NC group)(Table 1) were diagnosed according to the criteria of Hanifin and Rajka (4). All the pa-tients had clinically dry skin but no signs of eczema on the flexor surface of the forearm where skin hygroscopicity was measured.
The hydration status (i.e., water content) of the SC was measured with a corneometer (CM 820PC, Courage + Khazaka Electronics GmbH, Germany) in a room maintained at normal tem-perature (24°C) and relative humidity (55%). Measurement points were marked with a felt-tipped pen, with the marks placed a small dis-tance away from each measurement point so as not to affect the results. All subjects were al-lowed to acclimate to the examination room for 20 minutes before the start of the study.
Each subject’s right forearm was immersed in a tub filled with 10 L of comfortably hot water (40°C) containing a negligible concentration of
residual chlorine (i.e., less than 0.03 mg/L). This water was purified using an MJD0001 puri-fier (Sekisui Chemical Co., Ltd., Japan). Con-currently, the subject’s left forearm was im-mersed in a tub filled with 10 L of warm water at the same temperature containing residual chlo-rine at 0.5 mg/L. Both arms were immersed for a total of 10 minutes. The water containing residual chlorine at 0.5 mg/L was prepared by the addition of sodium hypochloride solution (Wako Pure Chemical Industries, Ltd., Japan), and the chlorine concentration was confirmed using a Pocket Colorimeter Chlorine (HACH Company, USA).
Following the 10-minute immersion period, both arms were removed from the tubs and dried with a cotton towel. The hydration status was measured immediately and then every 30 seconds for a period of 10 minutes.
One hour later, after confirming that the hy-dration status had returned to the pre-treatment level, the experiment was repeated using water containing residual chlorine at a concentration of 1.0 mg/L for the right forearm and 2.0 mg/L for the left forearm.
The water-holding capacity of the SC after im-mersion was then calculated by integration of the hydration status determined every 30 sec-onds over a period of 10 minutes.

Discussion The findings of the present study indi-
cate, first, that the water-holding capacity of the SC in patients with AD is more sensitive to free residual chlorine exposure than that in NC subjects without AD. Second, these results suggest that free residual chlorine exposure in patients with AD may play a role in the development or exacerbation of AD.
Keeping the skin clean is an important part of the management of patients with AD because sweat, dirt, and staphylococcus are
known to exacerbate skin conditions in these patients. Therefore, many dermatolo-gists recommend the patients with AD show-er or bathe every day. However, if patients with AD are more sensitive to residual chlo-rine, as we have shown in this study, bathing in tap water containing a high concentra-tion of chlorine should be avoided.
The Japanese Ministry of Health and Wel-fare (JMHW) prescribes that the free resid-ual chlorine in swimming water should be not less than 0.4 mg/L (ppm) and ideally not greater than 1.0 mg/L (5). On the other hand, with regard to tap water, JMHW provides guidelines for only the lower limit (0.1 mg/L), with no upper limit specified. Therefore, the residual chlorine concentra-tion in tap water ranges from 0.5 mg/L to 1.5 mg/L in various regions. In general, chlorine concentrations tend to be higher in urban areas and lower in rural areas. There are two possible ways to avoid using tap water containing a high concentration of residual chlorine for bathing. The first is to use special equipment to reduce the res-idual chlorine concentration in tap water, and the second is to use bath salts that coun-teract the effects of chlorine (6, 7). The dis-infection of tap water is necessary to ensure public health, and the residual chlorine level found in Japanese tap water is not harmful to the skin of normal healthy per-sons, as we have demonstrated. However, the results of the present study suggest that efforts should be made to determine the ap-propriate upper limit of free residual chlo-rine so as to both ensure the safety of tap water and minimize chlorine exposure in sensitive individuals. In order to reduce the levels of chlorine required to disinfect tap water, efforts should also be made to reduce environmental pollution so as to improve the water quality of the rivers and lakes used as sources of tap water (8, 9).
Comfortably hot (40°C) water was used in the present study in order to approximate the conditions during bathing, but the tem-perature of water in swimming pools is much lower. The barrier function of the SC

In addition, the mechanism by which free residual chlorine reduces the water-holding capacity of the SC was not investigated in the present study. The mechanism by which the SC holds water has been studied in re-cent years, and lipids within the SC have been reported to play an important role (10, 11). Oxidation by free residual chlorine may affect the structure of the SC. Further studies are needed to clarify the mechanism by which free residual chlorine reduces the water-holding capacity of the SC.
