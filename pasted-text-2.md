Abstract
Background Hardness of water is determined by the amount of calcium carbonate
(CaCO3) and magnesium sulfate (MgSO4) dissolved in it. Hardness of water used for
washing hair may damage the hair.
Objectives The objective of this study is to observe the surface changes due to hard
water usage and compare the thickness of hair between hard and soft water treated
samples.
Methods Ten to 15 hair strands of length 15–20 cm, which were lost during combing, were
obtained from 15 healthy female volunteers. Each hair sample was cut into two equal
halves to obtain two sets per volunteer. Each hair sample was wrapped around a glass
rod. One set of 15 samples was washed with hard water, and the other set was washed
with distilled water for 10 minutes on alternate days and air-dried. This procedure was
carried out for 30 days. The surface of hair treated in hard and soft water was examined
under a scanning electron microscope.
Results The CaCO3 and MgSO4 content of hard and distilled water samples were
determined as 212.5 ppm of CaCO3 and 10 ppm of CaCO3 respectively. The mean
calcium deposition in hard and distilled water treated hair was determined as 0.804% and
0.26%, respectively. The mean magnesium deposition in hard and distilled water treated
hair was determined as 0.34% and 0.078%, respectively. The mean thickness of hair
treated in hard water and distilled water were 72.78 and 78.14 lm, respectively.
Conclusion The surface of hard water treated hair has a ruffled appearance with higher
mineral deposition and decreased thickness when compared with the surface of distilled
water treated hair.
Introduction
Hair loss and damage is a common problem in many
parts of the world. Patients often implicate the use of
hard water for brittleness and dryness of hair. Hardness
of water is that characteristic that prevents lathering of
soap and stains water pipes, dishwashers, and utensils.
Water can be classified based on hardness (in mg/l as
CaCO 3 ; 1 mg/l = 1.001 ppm) as soft (0–60), moderate
(61–120), hard (121–180), and very hard (>180).1 Hard-
ness of water is imparted by the salts such as CaCO 3 and
MgSO4 and can be classified as temporary and perma-
nent. 2 Temporary hardness is due to bicarbonate salts dis-
solved in the water and can be removed by boiling
whereas permanent hardness is due to sulfate salts and
cannot be removed by boiling. The objectives of this
study were:
- To compare the surface changes of hair treated with
hard water and hair treated with soft water.
- To estimate and compare the mineral content deposited
on the hair treated with hard water and soft water.
- To compare the change in thickness due to hard water.
Methodology
Fifteen healthy female volunteers aged 20–25 were
selected, and 10–15 strands of hair, 15–20 cm in length,
that had fallen off while combing were obtained. The
tapered edges were cut, and the rest of the hair sample
was cut into two equal halves. Each hair sample was
wrapped around a glass rod, and the ends were tied
with nylon threads. One set was washed in hard water
every other day for 10 minutes and air-dried; the other
set was similarly washed with soft water. This proce-
dure was repeated for 30 days. The hardness of hard
water and soft water used was estimated using water
analysis by EDTA method.3 Five random samples were
selected and examined under scanning electron micro-
scope. The amount of calcium and magnesium deposited
on the hair and the surface changes on hair were stud-
ied. The thickness of hair samples were measured and
compared.

Results
Water analysis done to determine the amount of hardness
present in the water samples used in the study showed
that the hard water sample contained a total hardness of
212.5 ppm of CaCO 3 while the distilled water sample
had a total hardness of 10 ppm of CaCO 3. Additionally
the amount of calcium and magnesium contributing to
the total hardness was measured. The amount of calcium
present in hard and distilled water samples was deter-
mined as 97.5 and 7.5 ppm of CaCO 3 respectively. The
amount of magnesium in hard water and distilled water
samples was determined as 115 and 2.5 ppm of CaCO 3 ,
respectively. Under the scanning electron microscope the
mean calcium deposition on hard water and distilled
water treated hair was 0.804% and 0.26%, respectively.
The mean magnesium deposition on hard and distilled
water treated hair was 0.34% and 0.078%, respectively
(Table 1). The mean thickness of hair treated in hard
water and distilled water was 72.78 and 78.14 lm,
respectively (Table 2).
Discussion
Hair is a natural asset to humankind. It is believed that
hard water, when used for bathing purposes, has harmful
effects on hair. Hard water is water with high mineral
content and is believed to make the hair dry, rough, and
lack luster. A previous study has shown that the hardness
of water does not adversely affect the tensile strength and
elasticity of hair.4 This study was designed to study the
surface changes on the hair shaft due to hard water
usage, compared to soft water usage, using an electron
microscope. Additionally, the thickness of hair washed in
hard and soft water was estimated and compared. We

found that the mineral deposition is higher in hair treated
in hard water with a decrease in thickness when com-
pared to hair treated in soft water. Baseline electron
microscopic findings of hair surface could not be consid-
ered, as any hair sample obtained for the study was
washed in water with variable degrees of hardness, either
soft or hard, before the study.
Under a scanning electron microscope, the hard water
treated hair appears irregular with more mineral deposits
(Fig. 1). In soft water treated hair, the surface is relatively
uniform with less mineral deposits (Fig. 2). However, the
surface changes of hard water treated hair may not be
entirely attributed to the usage of hard water alone as
weathering may also play a role. The increased mineral
deposition may be responsible for the surface changes
seen in hard water treated hair. This leads to the postula-
tion that the salt deposits on the hair surface may act as
abrasives while washing the hair and cause cortical dis-
continuity and water loss, eventually resulting in decreased
thickness. Additionally the decrease in thickness of hard
water treated hair may be due to the hygroscopic nature
of the salts deposited on the surface, enabling absorption
of water from within the hair shaft.
Conclusion
Based on the above findings we conclude that hair
washed on a regular basis with hard water has a higher
mineral deposition, leading to irregularity and discontinu-
ity of the surface and decreased thickness.
