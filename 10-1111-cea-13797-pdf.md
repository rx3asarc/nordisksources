**1 ABSTRACT 2 3 Background**
4 Hard domestic water has been reported to worsen atopic eczema (AE) and may contribute to its
5 development in early life.
*6 7 Objective*
8 To review the literature on the relationship between the effect of water hardness (high calcium carbonate;
9 CaCO3) on a) the risk of developing AE, b) the treatment of existing AE, and c) skin barrier function in
10 human and animal studies.
*11 12 Design, data sources and eligibility criteria*
13 We systematically searched databases (MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL, GREAT and Web of
14 Science) from inception until 30/6/2020. Human and animal observational and experimental studies were
15 included. The primary outcomes were risk of AE and skin barrier function. Studies were meta-analysed
16 using a random-effects model. Evidence certainty was evaluated using the Grading of Recommendations,
17 Assessment, Development and Evaluations (GRADE) approach.
*18 19 Results*
20 Sixteen studies were included. Pooled observational data from 7 studies on 385,901 participants identified
21 increased odds of AE in children exposed to harder versus softer water (odds ratio 1.28, 95% CI 1.09, 1.50;
22 GRADE certainty: very low). Two mechanistic studies in humans reported higher deposition of the
23 detergent sodium lauryl sulphate in those exposed to harder versus softer water. Two randomized
24 controlled trials comparing water softeners with standard care did not show a significant difference in
25 objective AE severity with softened water (standardised mean difference 0.06 standard deviations higher,
26 95% CI 0.16 lower to 0.27 higher; GRADE certainty: moderate).
*27 28 Conclusions & Clinical Relevance*
29 There was a positive association between living in a hard-water (range: 76 to >350 mg/L CaCO3) area and
30 AE in children. There is no evidence that domestic water softeners improve objective disease severity in
31 established AE. There may be a role of water hardness in the initiation of skin inflammation in early life, but
32 there is a need for further longitudinal and interventional studies.
33 34 Systematic review registration: PROSPERO (CRD42016051528)

**1 INTRODUCTION 2 Atopic eczema (AE, syn. atopic dermatitis) is a chronic inflammatory skin condition with a multifactorial**
3 aetiology and a high level of disease burden among both adults and children. The rising incidence of AE in
4 many countries around the world over the past decades has raised the question of whether environmental
5 factors may be contributing to this growing public health problem.1 Given the ubiquity of bathing around the
6 world, there has long been an interest in the relationship between the water and wash products that we
7 bathe with and their effects on the skin.
8 9 Rainwater is naturally soft due to low mineral content. As rainwater falls to the ground and percolates
10 through the rock it dissolves minerals and may become hard. This is particularly the case when water
11 percolates through limestone where the water picks up calcium carbonate (CaCO3). Thus the level of water
12 hardness can vary considerably depending on the local geology and the proportion of groundwater that
13 constitutes the household water supply. The UK has a very wide range of water hardness levels: from very
14 hard water in south-east England (CaCO3 >400 mg/L) to very soft water in Scotland (≤60 mg/L CaCO3).
15 Such variation in water hardness levels can be observed even within small geographical proximity. For
16 instance, variation in the levels of water hardness across the county of Nottinghamshire was associated
17 with significant differences in the prevalence of AE among primary school children in the late 1990s.2
18 19 Consequently, a number of studies have sought to explore the hypothesis that skin exposure to hard water
20 leads to skin barrier dysfunction and an increased risk of AE. Various potential mechanisms for this have
21 been proposed, including a direct effect of calcium carbonate on the skin. As calcium has a natural gradient
22 in the epidermis, disruption of this gradient might lead to abnormalities in keratinocyte differentiation.3 In
23 culture, low concentrations of calcium stimulate the growth of primary keratinocytes and increasing calcium
24 concentrations promote terminal differentiation.4 Exogenous calcium applied to murine skin has been
25 shown to inhibit the repair process of the epidermis following acute barrier disturbance with organic
26 solvents.5 Calcium also has a role in the binding of lamellar bodies to the granular cell layer membranes
27 and, therefore, in the formation of the bilayer lipid water barrier. Disruption of this barrier is thought to be a
28 key step in the early development of AE.6 Furthermore, hard water is more alkaline than soft water resulting
29 in increased buffering capacity, i.e. the ability to resist pH changes. This means hard water may be able to
30 modify skin surface pH. Changes in pH influence skin barrier function through altering the activity of
31 epidermal enzymes such as the lymphoepithelial Kazal-type 5 serine protease inhibitor (LEKTI). LEKTI
32 cleaves profilaggrin into filaggrin as well as inhibiting kallikrein (KLK) proteases KLK5 (stratum corneum
33 tryptic enzyme, SCTE), KLK7 (stratum corneum chymotryptic enzyme, SCCE), and KLK14, important for
34 desquamation. LEKTI activity is enhanced in more acidic conditions.7 Additionally, there is a chemical
35 interaction between some surfactants in everyday wash products and hard water which results in increased
36 deposition of metallic surfactants on the skin.8 The repeated nature of such exposures may have a
37 cumulative detrimental effect on skin barrier integrity.

**1 METHODS 2 3 Literature search and study selection**
4 A systematic literature search was performed to answer the question ‘What is the effect of water hardness
5 on skin barrier function and AE?’. The conduct and reporting of this review followed the Preferred Reporting
6 Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) statement9 and the Meta-analyses of
7 Observational Studies (MOOSE) checklist.9,10 The protocol was registered with PROSPERO
8 (CRD42016051528). Systematic searches were conducted in MEDLINE, Embase, Cochrane CENTRAL,
9 GREAT and Web of Science (including the Conference Proceedings Citation Index) from inception to 30th
10 June 2020 using MESH terms such as ‘Eczema’ ‘Atopic dermatitis’ ‘water’ ‘water softening’ calcium’
11 ‘magnesium’ and combinations of related free text keywords (full search strategy see Supplementary
12 Appendix S1). Searches were performed by a researcher trained in systematic reviews (ZKJ-L). No
13 language restrictions were used. References of included studies were reviewed for additional papers of
14 relevance.
15 16 Inclusion and exclusion criteria
17 We included human and animal observational and experimental studies evaluating water hardness
18 (calcium carbonate), water softening and/or filtration devices vs. naturally soft water, softened water,
19 chlorine-free water, deionised water, filtered water. All study types were included, including conference
20 abstracts. There were no language restrictions and no other exclusion criteria.
21 22 Outcome measures
23 The primary outcomes were risk of AE and skin barrier function impairment, as measured by raised
24 transepidermal water loss (TEWL). No standardised definition of AE was specified. Secondary outcomes
25 were also extracted, including clinician-assessed AE severity, patient-reported AE severity, AE disease
26 control, time to onset of AE, wash product use including traditional alkyl carboxylate soaps and newer
27 synthetic detergents (syndets), biological and cellular measures of skin barrier function, such as cutaneous
28 cytokine release, and detergent deposition.
*29 30 Data abstraction and risk of bias assessment*
31 After de-duplication, abstracts and titles were screened independently by two different researchers (ZKJ-L
*32 and CYU). Full-text articles of selected titles/abstracts were reviewed against a priori defined inclusion and*
33 exclusion criteria, again independently by two different researchers (ZKJ-L and CYU). Discrepancies were
34 resolved by discussion with the senior author (CF). Data extraction and quality assessment were performed
35 by one researcher and checked by another against the original article.
36 37 Randomised controlled trial (RCT) risk of bias was assessed using the Cochrane Risk of Bias tool.11 Study

14 heterogeneity (I2 >50%) was identified, this was explored through analysis of pre-defined subgroups:
15 Infants (<1 year); primary school-aged children (5 to 11 years of age), secondary school-aged children (11-

25 considered statistically significant. We planned to assess publication bias using funnel plots for quantitative
26 syntheses of ≥10 studies.

*10 Hard water exposure and risk of atopic eczema*
11 The pooled estimate based on 385,901 participants in five cross-sectional and two birth cohort studies2,20-25

*17 18 Hard water exposure and risk of atopic eczema in infants*
19 Two studies22,25 reported a higher risk of AE in infants living in a hard water area and one23 reported no
20 statistically significant difference in AE risk between harder and softer water areas (Figure 2). These
21 studies adjusted for potential confounders, including geographical location (Table 1). The pooled estimate
22 based on 27,892 infants was almost identical to that for all children but was not significant (OR 1.29, 95%
23 CI 0.89, 1.86). Certainty in this estimate is very low due to high risk of bias and heterogeneity (I2 68%;

**1 DISCUSSION 2 We performed a systematic literature review and meta-analysis of the relationship between water hardness**
3 and the primary outcomes of AE and skin barrier function. Overall, in non-infant children exposed to harder
4 versus softer water, there were slightly increased odds of AE, although there is very low certainty in the
5 evidence. In infants and secondary school-aged children, there was no clear evidence of overall increased
*6 risk of AE with hard water exposure. However, infants with FLG mutations exposed to hard water may be at*
7 a higher risk of AE. In adults, hard water may result in skin barrier dysfunction, as measured by slight
8 increases in short-term TEWL and skin erythema, with a more marked effect seen in adults with AE who
*9 have FLG mutations. Hard water appears to result in little to no difference in skin IL-1α release or skin*
10 surface pH but may reduce short-term skin hydration slightly. Secondary outcomes of clinician- and patient-
11 reported AE severity and control were also reviewed. There is evidence that water softeners provide no
12 objective improvement in AE severity when used in addition to usual care, with low certainty in the
13 evidence. However, there is moderate-quality evidence that water softeners reduce patient-reported AE
14 severity slightly and improve AE disease control slightly in addition to usual care.
*15 16 Strengths and limitations*
17 We conducted a comprehensive systematic search with the inclusion of the 'grey' literature. We assessed
18 risk of bias for included studies and rated the certainty of the evidence for each outcome using the GRADE
19 approach. There were key methodological limitations in the studies of AE risk that preclude firm
20 conclusions being drawn regarding the role of hard water exposure on AE risk in children. There was a lack
21 of studies in very young children and in adults. Definitions of hard water and AE varied across studies,
22 potentially leading to misclassification. Most studies used questionnaire-based child- or parent-reported AE
23 to determine AE status, some of which were based on the validated ISAAC questionnaire. Exposure to
24 hard water is a product of water hardness and bathing frequency. Observational studies only look at the
25 association between living in a hard water area and developing AE, rather than the effect of hard water
*26 exposure per se on the risk of AE development. Some studies included adjustment for confounders,*
27 however, the studies were mainly of a cross-sectional design and therefore do not allow causal
28 interpretations to be made. Furthermore, the definition and nature of the confounders accounted for in the
29 different studies varied, and so as with any observational study, there is a risk of bias due to residual
30 confounding.
31 32 A limitation of this review is that single, rather than double, data extraction was used. There was a high
33 degree of heterogeneity between studies that persisted after subgroup analyses, suggesting that the results
34 must be interpreted with caution. An obvious difference between studies is in the levels of hard water
35 between study areas. It could be that the baseline is too high, and/or that the comparator is too low. Indeed,
36 removing the Miyake study where the comparison was between 70 mg/L CaCO3 and <48 mg/L CaCO3

*3 4 Interpretation of main findings*
5 The development of AE is multifactorial and likely due to genetic determinants of skin barrier function and
6 immunity along with environmental exposures that initiate and perpetuate the cycle of inflammation and
7 skin barrier damage seen in chronic AE. The potential effect of environmental exposures, such as hard
8 water, on skin barrier function has been hypothesised to be greater in early life because the skin barrier is
9 immature. Our finding of an increased risk of AE in younger rather than older children fits with this
10 hypothesis. Data from the Warren et al and Danby et al studies suggest that detergents in wash products
11 may interact with hard water resulting in deposition of detergents on the skin leading to a detrimental effect
12 on skin barrier function. Whilst the focus in the literature has been on calcium carbonate as the main
*13 determinant of water hardness, magnesium also influences hardness. Evidence from in vitro studies*
14 suggests that magnesium has a beneficial effect on skin barrier function. Furthermore, this effect may be
15 enhanced by the addition of calcium, so long as the ratio of calcium to magnesium is <1.32 Dead Sea water
16 has a very high calcium level (17,600 mg/L) yet it is reported as having beneficial properties for the skin.

19 cations such as iron, zinc, and manganese, this may partly explain the lack of efficacy of water softeners in

21 in that softened water retains the alkaline pH of the original hard water, whereas naturally soft water has a
22 lower (more acidic) pH due to lower alkalinity. The short-term data from the study by Danby et al, however,

24 which may be due to the innate buffering potential of the skin.8 Wash products have a major effect on skin

27 declined, probably leading to lower skin pH levels. This change in practice, in addition to varying early

29 considered when measuring the effect of water hardness on skin surface pH.

32 cause. However, hard water may be a component cause. For example, following an initial barrier insult,

3 hard water where there is repeated exposure at varying frequencies and duration and in combination with
4 multiple wash products that, again, may be used in varied ways.
*5 6 Implications for clinical practice and future research*
7 Water hardness needs to be considered in the context of other risk factors for the development of AE, both
8 genetic and environmental, particularly in younger children. Based on the evidence, it is unclear how the
9 evidence may be used practically for clinicians working in hard water areas. While there is good evidence
10 that water softeners do not help in the treatment of AE, there is an open question as to whether water
11 softeners may reduce the risk of AE in children. This review has highlighted a need for RCT evidence and
12 has helped to shape the design of a randomised controlled trial of water softeners for the prevention of AE
13 in newborn babies that is currently underway (ClinicalTrials.gov: NCT03270566).
*14 15 Conclusions*
16 Our findings have highlighted a lack of high-quality evidence on the effect of water hardness exposure on
17 AE risk in adults and very young children. There is evidence for an increased risk of AE in primary-school-
18 aged children exposed to harder water (range: 76 to >350 mg/L CaCO3). Water softeners provide no
19 additional objective benefit in the treatment of AE, however, they may provide subjective improvements in
20 AE severity. There may be a role of water hardness in the initiation of skin inflammation in early life, but
21 there is a need for further longitudinal and interventional studies to test this.
