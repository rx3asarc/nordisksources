Abstract
The present study reports the feasibility of synthesis of a new composite material con-
taining zeolite (ZSM-5) and activated carbon that was prepared from custard apple shell
powder. The process consisted of the following steps: preparation of activated carbon
and subsequent steam assisted dry gel conversion of tetraethyl orthosilicate and sodium
aluminate to ZSM-5 and ZSM-5 activated carbon composite. The resulting composite
materials were characterized by X-ray diffraction, scanning electron microscope, and
Brunauer–Emmett–Teller techniques. The co-ordination of the alumina incorporated
was analyzed using Al magic angle spinning nuclear magnetic resonance. A comparative
study among ZSM-5, ZSM-5 activated carbon composite, and mechanical mixture was
executed toward sequestration of Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions from aqueous solution. The ZSM-
5/activated carbon composite exhibited higher sequestration efficiency of lead (Pb2+)
and cadmium (Cd2+) from aqueous solution and further from industrial effluents com-
pared to other two sorbents. The ZSM-5/activated carbon composite exhibited selec-
tive adsorption toward Pb2+ ions compared to Cd2+ ions and other divalent metal ions.

1 | INTRODUCTION
Environmental pollution has emerged as major global concern due to its
adverse effects on the various ecosystems. Of various kinds of pollution,
water pollution is considered to be one of the most devastating pollution.1
Rapid industrialization and population explosion contribute for growing
water pollution. Various treatment techniques are available for the treat-
ment of wastewater, that is, released from industries in the form of efflu-
ents and municipal sewage water. Among many techniques, adsorption is
found to be one of the superior techniques for the wastewater remedia-
tion, especially for industrial effluents loaded with heavy metal ions and
synthetic dyes.2 Adsorbents such as activated carbon,3 agro-waste,1,2
nanosorbents,4 synthetic resins,5 and zeolites6 and so on, are investigated
and reported.Many adsorbents are found to be superior in various aspects;
however, lack in regeneration, eco-friendly and economical aspects.
Natural zeolites are crystalline hydrated aluminosilicates of alkali and
alkaline earth cations.7 Due to their unique physico-chemical properties
(e.g., high crystallinity, regular subnanometric cavities, highly selective
ion-exchange capacity), zeolites are being used as heavy metals sorbents,
as chemical sieves and as water softeners.8 The use of natural zeolite as
an adsorbent has gained interest among researchers as its sorption prop-
erties provide a combination of ion exchange and molecular sieve prop-
erties, which can also be easily modified.9 In addition, zeolites are widely
distributed in nature and are inexpensive and nontoxic sorbents. Besides
the natural zeolite, the synthesized zeolite and zeolite/activated carbon
composites are also active sorbents and play an important role in the
adsorption of many metal ions.9 Despite this interest, there is very little
literature published on the kinetics of the process. Kinetic studies are
important because the data obtained can be used for designing industrial
adsorption systems through modeling and simulation.

Several studies have been devoted to the synthesis protocols of
zeolites/activated carbon composites (particularly activated carbon pre-
pared from natural waste), proving that such materials are able to adsorb
high concentrations of heavy metal ions and synthetic dyes in addition to
their improved catalytic properties.10,11 It was found that the zeolite com-
posite adsorbed higher amounts of Cu2+, Cd2+, and Pb2+ than the
unmodified zeolite. Recently, in many studies, zeolites were used as sub-
strates formodification, to improve the sorption of lead (Pb2+) and cadmium
(Cd2+) in particular.12
In this study, the use of custard apple shell (CAS), a nonedible
waste found in many coastal areas of India was used for preparing
activated carbon and then the same was used to prepare
zeolite/activated carbon composite material. The zeolite/activated
carbon composite material was evaluated for its sorption capacity of
Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions from aqueous solutions and industrial effluent.
2 | MATERIALS AND METHODS
2.1 | Chemicals and reagents
All chemicals and reagents used are Analytical Grade reagents pro-
cured from Sigma-Aldrich and other standard chemical suppliers. Milli
Q water was used in all the studies. One thousand milligram per liter
stock solutions of Pb2+ and Cd2+ were prepared by dissolving appro-
priate amount of nitrate salts in Milli Q water. Adjustments of pH
were carried out using 0.1M HCl and 0.1M NaOH solutions.
2.2 | Experimental
CAS was milled and sieved to segregate particles with uniform size below
100 mesh British Standards Society (BSS) and then the uniform sized par-
ticleswere converted to activated carbon according to the standard proto-
cols.13,14 The obtained CAS-based activated carbon was stored in
separate polyethylene bottles for further use. To prepare ZSM-5 activated
carbon composite, the mixture containing 10 ml of TEOS, 3 g of NaAlO2,
and 2 g Activated Carbon (AC) was stirred overnight followed by drying at
80C for 12 hr. After drying, a gel was obtainedwhichwas added to 15 ml
of tetra propyl ammonium hydroxide, mixed by stirring and dried over-
night at 100C. The dried gel was kept in a vial which was then placed in
an autoclave containing 2 ml of water, and that was kept in an oven at
170C for 72 hr. The 2 ml of water was added that generated the steam
which assisted in the dry gel conversion. The resulting dried sample was
calcined in a tubular furnace at 550C for 3 hr underN2 flow (150 ml/min).
The mechanical mixture (MM) was prepared by adding AC in 50 wt% to
the prepared ZSM-5 and was ground for 8–10 hr. The resultant
zeolite/activated carbon composite were characterized by X-ray diffrac-
tometer (XRD), magic angle spinning nuclear magnetic resonance (Al MAS
NMR) and scanning electronmicroscope (SEM).
2.3 | Analytical methods
The crystallinity of the samples was determined by XRD (Bruker D8
Advance). Textural characterization was determined by adsorption
isotherms using Brunauer–Emmett–Teller (BET) using an automatic
adsorption system (Micromeritics ASAP 2020). Prior to this measure-
ment, the samples were degassed at 320C for 4 hr. The surface mor-
phology of the prepared zeolite composite materials is studied with
SEM (Philips XL30). Flame atomic absorption spectrophotometer
(Varian AA240) equipped with air/acetylene burner was used for deter-
mining the Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions concentrations from aqueous solution.
2.4 | Batch mode adsorption studies
Sorption experiments for prepared sorbents were studied individually at
room temperature in a mechanical shaker with 250 ml conical flasks.
Effect of parameters like adsorbent dosage, contact time, pH, and initial
metal concentration on the process of adsorption were studied. Sorption
capacity of the prepared sorbents was determined by contacting opti-
mized levels of sorbent with 20 ml of known Pb2+ and Cd2+ metal solu-
tions. The influence of pH on adsorption was evaluated by varying the
pH from 2 to 10 using 0.1M HCl and 0.1M NaOH for pH adjustment.
The influence of contact time was varied between 5 and 120 min. The
residual metal concentration present in the supernatant was determined
by atomic absorption spectrophotometer (AAS). Each experiment was
repeated thrice and average values have been reported. The amount of

2.5.2 | Desorption studies
Regeneration and reuse of adsorbent is considered to be imperative
parameter which determines the cost of the treatment process. To
access the regenerative and reuse capability of the present adsorbents,
desorption studies were investigated with HCl as desorbing agents.
2.5.3 | Multimetal sorption studies
In general, the industrial effluents contain various types of metal ions
and these metal ions may exhibit competitive adsorption for the surface
active sites for binding. To understand the competitive adsorption pro-
cess, a study was executed for the present adsorbents in a multimetal
system for the removal of Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions. Metal ions such as Cu2+,
Co2+, and Zn2+ ions were selected for the study of multimetal system.
Multimetal experiments were carried out in 50 ml of test solution con-
taining 50 mg/L concentration of all the selected metal ions.

solution; at pH higher than pHpzc, sorbent surface is negatively
charged and could interact with metal species, while at pH lower than
pHpzc, solid surface is positively charged and could interact with neg-

3.5.2 | Effect of adsorbent dose
The adsorbent dose is one other important parameter that needs to
be optimized; as it contributes to the economic value of the adsorp-
tion process. To optimize the adsorbent dose, series of experiments
were conducted by varying the adsorbent dose at constant pH and
initial metal ion concentration. It was observed that removal efficiency
increased with increase in adsorbent dose of all the three adsorbents
ZSM-5, ZSM-5/AC, and MM (Figure 5a,b). However, the loading

removal efficiency for ZSM-5, ZSM-5/AC, and MM were observed at

composite material exhibited higher removal efficiency at lower
adsorbent dosage. The efficiency observed is due to the availability of
ample pores on the surface of the composite materials, which facili-
tates accommodation of copious metal ions. Further investigations are
carried out at the optimized adsorbent dose.
3.6 | Kinetics of adsorption
Kinetics of the adsorption process was studied for all the three adsor-
bent with respect to contact time. Sorption experiments were con-
ducted at different time intervals (10–120 min) for removal of Pb2+
and Cd2+ ions onto ZSM-5, ZSM-5/AC, and MM. The uptake of Pb2+
and Cd2+ was rapid initially and equilibrium had been achieved within
60 min. The resulting data was analyzed with two well-known kinetic
models such as pseudo first- and pseudo second-order kinetic models.
The obtained correlation coefficients and constants for the two
models are summarized in Table 2. It is observed that the correlation
coefficients obtained for the pseudo first-order kinetic model for all the
three studied adsorbents are very low suggesting the failure of the
model to explain the adsorption process. The failure of the model is fur-
ther supported by the experimental loading capacities not overlapping
with the theoretical values. The correlation coefficients obtained for
pseudo second order is very high compared to pseudo first order and
almost close to one. The plots of pseudo second-order model obtained
for the removal of Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions is represented in Figure 6. The
correlation coefficients being close to one suggest the better fit of the
model to the experimental data. Further, the better fit is supported by
the similarities in theoretical and experimental loading capacities. These
observations suggest that the pseudo second-order kinetic model bet-
ter explains the sequestration of Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions onto the ZSM-5,
ZSM-5/AC, and MM adsorbents. In general, the experimental data that
fits to pseudo second-order model indicate that the rate-limiting step
for the process involves chemical reaction, that is, chemisorption.23
Further, the kinetic data was analyzed with intraparticle diffusion
model to understand the mass transfer steps of adsorption of Pb2+
and Cd2+ ions by ZSM-5, ZSM-5/AC, and MM. The plots are repre-
sented in Figure 7a,b. It is observed that the plots of ZSM-5 for the
removal of Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions resulted in straight line, suggesting the
intraparticle diffusion of ions onto ZSM-5. The plots of ZSM-5 and
MM for Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions was found to be a two-step process. The
first linear plot is due to the immediate utilization of ample active sites
on the adsorbent surface and the second linear plot attributed to very
slow diffusion of the adsorbate from the surface site into the inner
pores.24 Thus, the initial adsorption of Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions by ZSM-5
and MM may be governed by intraparticle transport of surface diffu-
sion, and the later part may be controlled by pore diffusion.24

3.9 | Desorption studies
For revival of adsorbent, 0.1 g of Pb2+- and Cd2+-loaded adsorbents
were kept in contact with 20 ml of 0.1M HCl for 30 min by agitation
and desorbed acidic solution was analyzed by AAS to determine the
residual metal ion concentration. Desorption of Pb2+ and Cd2+ from
the metal-loaded adsorbents is presented in Figure 9. It can be seen
that the adsorbents can be effectively recycled for two cycles with-
out affecting the efficiency. A maximum desorption efficiency of
95% was achieved for all the three adsorbents for two cycles. The
adsorption efficiency is observed to reduce in the subsequent cycles
due to weakening of adsorption sites as a result of repeated acid
contact. However, desorption efficiency was found to be more than
75% even after five cycles of desorption investigations toward Pb2+
ions and for Cd2+ ions it is observed to be more than 80%. It is
understood that the desorption efficiency of ZSM-5/AC composite
is high compared to other two adsorbents beyond third cycle. Sev-
eral researchers have investigated desorption of adsorbed heavy
metal ions from zeolites. Desorption of Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions from
Iranian natural zeolite is found to be only 0.37 and 18% due to irre-
versible sorption reactions.25 In contrast to the above, the synthe-
sized zeolites and composites exhibited 95% desorption efficiency
which depicts the superiority of the present adsorbents in regenera-
tion process.
3.10 | Loading capacity comparison
The loading capacities of the present adsorbents were compared with
the literature and the results are summarized in Table 5. It was
observed that the loading capacities of the ZSM-5, ZSM-5/AC, and
MM are found to have higher loading capacities compared to many
other adsorbents reported in literature. It is lucid from Table 5 that
the unmodified zeolites exhibited low-adsorption capacity toward
heavy metal ions compared to surface modified zeolites. The magnetic
and nanoiron supported zeolites were found to exhibit higher adsorp-
tion capacity than unmodified zeolites.28,29 The present synthesized
ZSM-5/AC found to have higher efficiency compared to magnetic and
nanoiron supported zeolites. These observations establish the superi-
ority of the synthesized adsorbents over reported adsorbents.

remove Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions from the industrial effluent at optimized
conditions. The effluent samples were collected from metal processing
and battery industries located in Ranipet, Vellore district, and Tam-
ilnadu, India. The metal processing industries carryout forging activities
and battery units manufacture various types of heavy duty batteries for
commercial purposes. The above units indulge in use of various types
of heavy metal, which are generally observed in their effluents and
these effluents are used for the present study. The collected effluent
was filtered to remove all the insoluble portions and the filtrate was
taken for study with 10 time diluted samples. The Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions
concentration in the effluent was measured to be 25.5 and 21.3 mg/L
and the pH of the effluent were 3.5. For the treatment of this effluent
by ZSM-5, ZSM-5/AC, and MM the optimized condition were used. It
can be observed that the removal efficiency approximately 98 and 90%
of Pb2+ and Cd2+ by ZSM-5/AC; 92 and 90% of Pb2+ and Cd2+ by
ZSM/5; 80 and 88% of Pb2+ and Cd2+ by MM was achieved. As the
prepared adsorbents are biodegradable, it can be easily disposed after
recovering Pb2+ and Cd2+ ions from the adsorbent by desorption
process.34,35
