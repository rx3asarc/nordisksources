## Abstract
### Access to clean water remains a challenge in many parts of Africa. This is compounded by
### the increasing pollution levels with increasing population and industrialization.
### Activated carbon remains the most widely used adsorbent for the removal of chemical
### pollutants in both drinking water and wastewater treatment processes due to its unique
### chemical properties and a plethora of precursor materials for development. However, to
### date, no available literature has systematically summarized the advances in Africa on the
### adsorptive removal of contaminants using activated carbons and research issues such as
### data acquisition and presentation, analysis approaches, citations, inconsistencies and
### adsorption experimental setups remain largely undocumented. In this paper, adsorption

### contaminants are reviewed, from 2007 to 2022. The study focused on a literature-based
### approach by searching key terms in selected databases. The adsorption data were mostly
### best described by the Langmuir and pseudo-second-order models for dyes, anions, metal

## Introduction
### Activated carbon also commonly known as Activated charcoal is defined differently by
### several authors as observed in the literature. It is defined as a category of carbon-based
### that defies classification through structural formula or chemical analysis [1]. Further, AC
### is a class of carbon with a greater degree of porosity and extended inter-particle surface
### area [2]. Other researchers have defined activated carbon as a porous material obtained
### from carbon-rich feedstock with countless minuscule gaps between carbon atoms that
### have been developed through the activation process [3]. Activated carbons are the most
### preferred adsorbents for different industrial applications due to excellent sorption
### properties such as large surface area, superior porosity, high physicochemical stability
### and surface functional groups [4,5]. They are therefore considered good adsorbents for
### the removal of different chemical pollutants such as inorganic and organic substances
### from liquid and gas phases. The precursors for the synthesis of commercial activated
### carbons are petroleum, coke, coal, peat, and refinery residues, among others [3].
### However, these reported resources are non-renewable in nature and the resulting
### activated carbon is relatively expensive due to cost of production, consequently limiting
### its widespread usage in developing countries. In search of cheap and low-cost activated
### carbons investigators have successfully derived activated carbons from agro-wastes or
### industrial byproducts rich in elemental carbon [6,7,8,9].
### In general, activated carbons may be prepared by physical and chemical activations

### and ZnCl ) results to increased micropore volumes and surface areas. This is attributed to
### more extensive reaction between phosphoric acid (activator) and the surface of the

### carbonization process creating microspores and mesopores [9]. Under similar treatment
### conditions, the BET surface area and porosity structure also vary with the type of
### activator. For example, ZnCl  activated carbon exhibited a higher surface area (616 m /g)
### than activated carbon prepared using phosphoric acid (295 m /g) [18]. This shows the
### role of the properties of the activating agent and carbon-activator specific interactions in
### controlling textural properties and porosity structures. For pyrolysis temperature, a
### decrease in surface area with increase in pyrolysis temperature was attributed to the
### release of alkali and alkaline earth metals (AAEM) blocking the pores [17]. Additionally,
### increase in pyrolysis temperature decreased the micropore volume leading to increase in
### mesopore and macropore volumes [17]. This is due to the destruction of the walls
### separating the pores resulting in enlargement of the pores. Table 1 shows literature from

### cannot be determined a priori from the preparation conditions and must be obtained
### empirically. For instance, an increase in pyrolysis temperature can result in both an
### increase or decrease in surface area [17]. The interplay between process conditions and
### biomass composition, which vary infinitely, is a complex phenomenon that cannot be
### predicted using deterministic approaches. In the absence of a unifying scientific theory
### linking these parameters, machine learning tools present an opportunity for the
### development of predictive models linking the composition of the precursor, synthesis

### The majority of the people in the African continent do not have access to safe and clean
### water resources. Several communities are dependent on untreated surface water
### resources such as rivers, lakes and streams for domestic and drinking purposes [19].
### These sources are often contaminated with disease-causing pathogens making them
### breeding grounds for waterborne diseases. Research studies conducted in Africa have
### reported waterborne diseases like cholera, typhoid, and dysentery pose a significant
### threat to the public health and well-being of African people [20,21,22,23,24,25]. These
### diseases can lead to severe illness and death, particularly among vulnerable populations,
### including children and the elderly. Implementing water treatment measures is crucial to
### reducing the incidence of these diseases and protecting the health of the population.
### Addressing these challenges through water treatment is not only a necessity but also a
### critical step toward improving the overall quality of life for millions of African people.
### Therefore, monitoring progress and advances in water treatment in Africa is critical.
### Environmental pollution has emerged as a major global issue due to its severe effects
### health and associated ecological risks. Among the environmental arenas, water resources
### have gained much attention due to their prominence and concern for water quality [26].
### Of various kinds of pollution, water pollution is regarded to be one of the most
### disturbing pollution [27,28]. Manfrin et al. [29] reported that the United Nations in the
### year 2015 launched the 2030 Agenda, which among others introduced; 17 Sustainable
### Development Goals, the assurance to provide safe drinking water and sanitation.
### Efficiency in water treatment stages is characterized by the removal of hazardous
### pollutants, heavy metals [30], dyes, pesticides, and organic pollutants are effectively
### removed as indicated in the literature [18].
### Activated carbon is extensively applied in several fields such as in solvent recovery, air
### pollution control, and the treatment of industrial discharges which pose major health
### threats [31]. It has also been reported to be used for electrical energy applications,
### greenhouse gas adsorption, selective carbon dioxide capture and adsorption, hydrogen
### storage, as ultra-capacitors and purification of chemical contaminants in water [32,33].
### Though activated carbons have been used for water purification for a long time, to date,
### no available literature has systematically summarized the advances in Africa on the
### adsorptive removal of contaminants using activated carbons and highlighted any
### emerging issues, if any. Therefore, this paper focused on studies conducted between
### 2007 and 2022 in the African continent reporting the use of activated carbon for the
### sequestration of various classes of water pollutants. Adsorption data analysis approaches,
### experimental methods used, limitations, gaps and opportunities for further studies are
### highlighted.
## Methodology
### The methodology employed for this research paper involved a comprehensive process of
### accessing and analysing peer-reviewed academic papers. This methodology allows the
### inclusion of studies conducted in different geographical regions in Africa, thereby
### allowing a broader and more diverse perspective on the use of activated carbon for water
### treatment. Research articles were accessed from different scientific databases (PubMed
### and Google Scholar), and literature repositories (Research Gate). The articles were
### filtered to remove duplicated papers. The search keywords included "Activated carbon,
### pharmaceuticals, pesticides, PFOS, emerging pollutants, anions, heavy metals, country
### name" and the publication period was limited to between 2007 and 2022. The
### downloaded articles were critically analysed and reviewed.

## Copper ions
### South African activated carbon prepared by KOH chemical activation from green
### vegetable waste was used for the removal of copper ions in an aqueous solution. Several
### working parameters were studied and the outcome demonstrated successful elimination.
### The adsorption data fed into the Langmuir model produced excellent results with a
### maximum adsorption capacity of 75.0 mg/g implying a monolayer adsorption
### phenomenon [34]. However, the textural properties, namely surface area and pore
### structure, were not reported. Characterization entailed functional group identification
### (FTIR), thermal stability (TGA) and surface morphology (SEM). The overall results
### suggested that the vegetable green waste is a low-cost precursor for synthesizing
### activated carbon. In a related study, Nigerian investigators developed activated carbon
### using H PO  activating agent from African palm fruit and utilized it for the removal of
### copper ions from wastewater. With an initial concentration of 1.52 mg L , the developed
### activated carbon successfully removed 96.71 % at an optimal contact time of 60 min [35].
### However, the maximum adsorption capacity was not reported. The adsorbent was
### characterized in terms of bulk density and conductivity. A similar study conducted on
### simulated waste by Vunain et al. [8] in Malawi reported that activated carbon from

### capacity was recorded despite the large BET surface area of 1089 m /g. The study shows
### that the adsorption density for an activated carbon material for copper ions is not

## Lead ions
### Nigerian researchers used African palm fruit shells as precursors for preparing low-cost
### activated carbon. The efficacy of activated carbon demonstrated the elimination of
### 97.57 % of lead ions in 60 min from effluent originating from the scrap metal market with
### an initial concentration of 2.62 mg/L [35]. The percentage removal increased with
### indicating an endothermic process. The activated carbon was characterized in terms of
### conductivity and bulk density only, an arguably insufficient material characterization for
### new adsorbing material. The operating adsorption mechanism, kinetics and isothermal
### modelling and thermodynamic parameters were not reported. A 56.1 % removal of lead
### ions from petroleum refinery wastewater in Nigeria by a biosurfactant-loaded activated
### carbon prepared from agricultural wastes (rice husk), from an initial concentration of
### 0.57 to 0.22 mg/L was reported by Onuzulike et al. [46]. Freundlich model and pseudo-
### first-order gave the best fit for the experimental adsorption and kinetic data,
### correspondingly. Notably, the adsorbent was characterized by FTIR only. This limited
### reporting of characterization data is possibly due to limited funding and access to
### instrumentation. In a similar study carried out in Zimbabwe, maize tassel was turned
### into activated carbon for the uptake of lead ions [47]. Adsorbent characterization entailed
### bulk density, specific surface area (BET), loss of mass on ignition, pH, moisture content,
### functional groups (FTIR) and mineralogy (XRD). The results obtained showed a
### remarkable reduction of lead ions (96.6 %), achieving a Langmuir monolayer adsorption
### capacity of 37.31 mg/g in 30 min equilibrium time and pH 5.4. The specific surface area
### was 250 m /g. Different concentrations (0.05, 0.1, 0.2 and 0.3 M) of HCl were tested as
### desorption agents. The desorption rate increased with an increase in HCl concentration
### but attained a constant at 0.2 M HCl. In Nigeria, similar studies on lead ions reported that
### they were removed from tannery effluent with cobalt ferrite-supported activated carbon
### prepared from almond husks. With an initial concentration of 0.8942 mg/L, the
### maximum removal rate of 96.4 % of lead ions was attained. The maximum adsorption
### capacity achieved in the study was 6.27 mg/g. The best-fitting models were determined
### using the coefficient of determination (R ) and the sum of square error (SSE) values. The
### kinetic data generated in the study agreed well with the pseudo-second-order kinetic
### model. Based on the thermodynamic data and the mean adsorption energy (E), the
### adsorption of lead was determined to be a physical process [42]. The related study which
### used activated carbon developed from Moringa oleifera seed reported 100 percent
### removal of lead ions from borehole water with an initial adsorbate concentration of
### 0.65 mg/L [48].

## Conclusion
### The study focused on adsorption studies carried out in the African continent, between
### 2007 and 2022, using activated carbons for the removal of water contaminants. The
### adsorption data were mostly best described by the Langmuir and pseudo-second-order
### models. The highest adsorption capacity was 1552.5 mg/g for H PO -activated maize
### tassel-based carbon.
### According to the pooled data, the majority of the studies relied on linearized adsorption
### isotherm and kinetic models with coefficient of determination (R ) as the sole criterion
### for determining best-fitting models despite the limitations of this approach. Additionally,
### the majority of the studies were batch mode and laboratory-scale studies with no
### desorption and recyclability evaluations. Pilot-scale and life cycle assessment (LCA) of
### the reported new materials are aspects largely unexplored.
### While most classes of water pollutants are covered, emerging contaminants such as
### artificial sweeteners and new COVID-19 treatment drugs, doping substances among
### others, that have recently been reported in water resources should be considered. While
### most studies consider pollutants in single-solute solutions, in real wastewater, the
