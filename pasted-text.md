
Influence of free residual chlorine on cultured human epidermal
keratinocytes from normal skin and hypertrophic scars

Abstract
In Japan, public health regulations state that the water in rinsing pools used before swimming should contain 50- 100
mg/l of chlorine. We examined the influence of chlorination at high concentrations in rinsing pools on the skin using
cultured human epidermal keratinocytes from normal skin and hypertrophic scars.Chlorination of cell culture for 15
min with 200 mg/l of free residual chlorine proved cytotoxic to both types of keratinocytes as did 100 mg/l of free resid-
ual chlorine for 1 or 3 consecutive days. Keratinocytes from hypertrophic scars,when cultivated in 100 mg/l of free
residual chlorine, were more vulnerable to chlorine than those from normal skin. Cell characteristics of cultured
keratinocytes from hypertrophic scarsmay be somewhat different from those of normal skin. The phenomena observed
in this experimental model of the skin suggest that people exposed to chlorine in rinsing pools at concentrations in
excessof 200 mg/l for about 15 min before swimming are at risk of developing cutaneous disorders, especiallyat sites
of injury, e.g. scars.
Keywords: Chlorine; Cytotoxicity; Keratinocyte; Hypertrophic scar

2.2. Chlorination
For disinfection of domestic drinking water by
chlorination, it is said that an effective chlorine
concentration of l-2 mg/l will suffice [lo]. The
present study was conducted to examine the influ-
ence of chlorination at chlorine concentrations as
high as 50-100 mg/l upon both intact and damag-
ed human skin using cultured epidermal keratino-
cytes from normal skin and hypertrophic scars in
serum-free keratinocyte growth medium (K-GM).

Tatnall et al. (1990) [9] reported the cytotoxic
effects of hydrogen peroxide, sodium hypochlorite
and chlorhexidine, examined on cultured human
tibroblasts and basal keratinocytes in comparison
to those on a transformed keratinocyte line. At
therapeutic concentrations, all of these antiseptics
were 100% cytotoxic to all cell types. The concen-
tration allowing 50% survival of cells for sodium
hypochlorite tested on basal keratinocytes was
1.7 x 10s4 mol (126.5 mg/l) with an exposure
time of 15 min.
We needed l-day exposure at the concentration
of 100 mg/l of free residual chlorine to achieve a
50% survival of keratinocytes from hypertrophic
scars and 3-day exposure at the same concentra-
tion of free residual chlorine for keratinocytes.
from normal skin. Tatnall et al. [9] cultured kera-
tinocytes in medium which consisted of a 3: 1 mix-
ture of DMEM and Ham’s F12 medium with 5%
FCS, while we cultured the keratinocytes in serum-
free K-GM medium. The difference in medium
used may account for the different exposure time
required to obtain 50% survival of cells. Regard-
less of these time course differences, these data
showed that about 100 mg/l of free residual ch-
lorine was strongly cytotoxic to cultured
keratinocytes.
Kohno and Yoshii [14] reported that in order
for foot washes and rinsing pools before swimming
to be effective in disinfecting perianal dirt (fecal
material), water used for that purpose needs to be
chlorinated to such a degree as yields a free residu-
al chlorine concentration of 50-100 mg/l.
Actually, however, it seems very difficult to
maintain that level of chlorine in the water with
certainty, since the bactericidal capacity of a rins-
ing pool at swimming facilities is usually quickly
diminished through a loss of free chlorine caused
by its reactions with ammoniacal compounds
generated by the passage of many people through
the water. Chlorine first reacts with ammonia and
forms chloramines, or combined chlorine, which
are far less effective as antibacterial agents than
chlorine. Free chlorine possesses about 30 times
the antibacterial efficacy of chloramines. Hence,
chlorine needs to be added in sufficient concentra-
tions to destroy chloramines [ 151. Also, evapora-
tion of chlorine causes its concentration to be
decreased rapidly, especially outdoors during the
hot season. Moreover, free residual chlorine is
unstable to ultraviolet light [16,17].
In order to maintain the chlorine level of water
at 50-100 mg/l, more than that level of chlorine
needs to be added, and this in its turn may lead to
hazardous exposure to levels of chlorine higher

than 50-100 mg/l. When the concentration of
chlorine in a rinsing pool reaches over 200 mg/l,
even for a brief period, people exposed to this
water with chlorine remaining for about 15 min on
the excoriated skin before swimming are at risk of
developing skin disorders. From the dermatolog-
ical point of view, it can thus be stressed that un-
duly heavy chlorination of water aimed solely at
enhancing disinfectant eff’cacy is a potential
health hazard and should therefore be cautioned
against.
